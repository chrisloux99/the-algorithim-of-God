# Chapter 5: Blockchain and AI in Real-World Applications

The philosophical discourses explored in previous chapters find concrete expression in the real-world applications of blockchain and artificial intelligence. These technologies are no longer speculative; they are being deployed across a range of sectors, transforming how we manage health, conduct business, track goods, and govern societies. Each application carries its own set of practical challenges and ethical implications, reflecting the broader questions about trust, autonomy, and human flourishing that animate our inquiry. By examining these use cases, we can see how the theoretical debates about technology and belief play out in the messy, contingent world of human practice.¹

---

## 5.1. Healthcare

The healthcare sector offers a particularly vivid illustration of how blockchain and AI can converge to improve human well-being while raising complex ethical questions. The promise is significant: more accurate diagnoses, personalised treatments, secure sharing of medical records, and accelerated drug discovery. Yet the path to realising this promise is fraught with technical, regulatory, and moral challenges.²

### 5.1.1. Medical Records and Data Privacy

One of the most immediate applications of blockchain in healthcare is the management of medical records. Traditional systems of health data storage are often fragmented, insecure, and vulnerable to breaches.³ Blockchain offers a decentralised, immutable ledger that can secure patient records while allowing authorised access across different healthcare providers.⁴ When combined with AI, which can analyse these records to identify patterns and predict outcomes, the result is a system that enhances both the security and the utility of health information.⁵

However, this convergence also raises profound concerns about privacy and consent. Patients may be uncomfortable with their most intimate data being stored on a distributed ledger, even if it is encrypted.⁶ The potential for re-identification of anonymised data, the risk of data being used for purposes beyond the original consent, and the question of who controls the data are all pressing issues.⁷ Moreover, the use of AI to make inferences from health data—predictions about future illnesses, behavioural tendencies, or genetic predispositions—can feel invasive, challenging traditional notions of medical confidentiality and patient autonomy.⁸

### 5.1.2. AI-Driven Diagnostics and Treatment

AI algorithms have shown remarkable promise in diagnosing diseases from medical images, predicting patient outcomes, and recommending personalised treatment plans.⁹ When these algorithms are trained on data stored and verified on a blockchain, the integrity and provenance of the training data can be ensured, reducing the risk of bias or manipulation.¹⁰ This combination has the potential to democratise access to high-quality diagnostics, particularly in underserved regions where specialist physicians are scarce.¹¹

Yet the deployment of AI in clinical decision-making also raises questions about accountability and trust. When an algorithm recommends a course of treatment, who bears responsibility if something goes wrong?¹² How should clinicians and patients interpret probabilistic outputs that may lack clear causal explanations?¹³ These questions are not merely technical; they touch on fundamental issues of human agency, the nature of expertise, and the moral obligations of care.¹⁴

### 5.1.3. Drug Discovery and Clinical Trials

The process of discovering and testing new drugs is notoriously slow, expensive, and prone to failure.¹⁵ AI can accelerate this process by analysing vast datasets of molecular structures, predicting drug interactions, and identifying promising candidates for further testing.¹⁶ Blockchain can enhance the integrity of clinical trials by providing an immutable record of trial protocols, data, and results, thereby reducing the risk of fraud and ensuring transparency.¹⁷

Together, these technologies could shorten the time to market for life-saving medications and reduce costs.¹⁸ However, they also raise ethical concerns about access and equity. Will the benefits of AI-driven drug discovery be available to all, or will they be captured by wealthy nations and corporations?¹⁹ How should the intellectual property rights be managed when algorithms contribute to the discovery process?²⁰ These questions demand careful consideration as we navigate the intersection of innovation and justice.

## 5.2. Finance

The financial sector has been an early and enthusiastic adopter of blockchain and AI, drawn by the promise of greater efficiency, transparency, and security. From cryptocurrency and decentralised finance to algorithmic trading and fraud detection, these technologies are reshaping how we think about money, value, and trust.²¹

### 5.2.1. Cryptocurrency and Decentralised Finance

Cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum were among the first applications of blockchain technology, offering a decentralised alternative to traditional fiat currencies.²² These digital assets rely on cryptographic protocols to secure transactions and on consensus mechanisms to validate them, eliminating the need for intermediaries like banks.²³ Decentralised finance (DeFi) extends this principle to a wide range of financial services—lending, borrowing, insurance—creating an open, permissionless financial system accessible to anyone with an internet connection.²⁴

The implications are profound. DeFi has the potential to democratise access to financial services, particularly for the unbanked and underbanked populations of the world.²⁵ However, it also introduces new risks: the volatility of cryptocurrencies, the prevalence of scams and hacks, and the lack of regulatory oversight.²⁶ Moreover, the environmental impact of energy-intensive consensus mechanisms like proof-of-work has raised concerns about the sustainability of these systems.²⁷

### 5.2.2. Algorithmic Trading and Market Prediction

AI algorithms are increasingly used to execute trades at speeds and volumes far beyond human capability, analysing market data, news sentiment, and social media trends to predict price movements.²⁸ When these algorithms operate on blockchain-based platforms, the transparency and immutability of transaction records can enhance accountability and reduce the potential for market manipulation.²⁹

However, the rise of algorithmic trading also raises questions about market fairness and stability. The use of AI to gain competitive advantages can exacerbate inequalities between large institutional investors and individual retail investors.³⁰ Flash crashes, triggered by algorithmic interactions, have demonstrated the potential for these systems to destabilise markets.³¹ These events underscore the need for robust regulatory frameworks and ethical guidelines to ensure that the benefits of AI in finance are shared equitably and that systemic risks are managed.³²

### 5.2.3. Fraud Detection and Risk Management

Both blockchain and AI offer powerful tools for detecting and preventing fraud. Blockchain's immutable ledger makes it difficult to alter transaction records without detection, while AI can analyse patterns of behaviour to identify suspicious activity in real time.³³ Together, they can create more secure and trustworthy financial systems.³⁴

Yet the use of AI in risk management also raises concerns about bias and discrimination. Algorithms trained on historical data may perpetuate existing biases, leading to unfair outcomes for certain groups.³⁵ Moreover, the opacity of some AI models—often referred to as the "black box" problem—makes it difficult to understand how decisions are reached, challenging principles of transparency and due process.³⁶ These issues highlight the importance of developing AI systems that are not only effective but also fair, explainable, and accountable.³⁷

## 5.3. Supply Chain Management

The global supply chain is a complex network of producers, suppliers, distributors, and retailers, spanning multiple jurisdictions and involving a vast array of goods and services.³⁸ Ensuring the integrity, transparency, and efficiency of this network is a major challenge, one that blockchain and AI are uniquely positioned to address.³⁹

### 5.3.1. Provenance and Traceability

Blockchain's ability to create an immutable record of transactions makes it ideal for tracking the provenance of goods. From food products to luxury items, consumers increasingly want to know where their products come from and how they were made.⁴⁰ A blockchain-based system can provide a transparent, tamper-proof record of a product's journey from raw material to finished good, enhancing trust and accountability.⁴¹

AI complements this capability by analysing data from across the supply chain to identify inefficiencies, predict disruptions, and optimise logistics.⁴² For example, AI algorithms can forecast demand, manage inventory, and route shipments in real time, reducing waste and improving customer satisfaction.⁴³ Together, these technologies can create supply chains that are not only more transparent but also more resilient and responsive.⁴⁴

### 5.3.2. Ethical Sourcing and Sustainability

Consumers and regulators are increasingly demanding that companies demonstrate ethical sourcing practices and environmental responsibility.⁴⁵ Blockchain can help by providing verifiable records of sourcing conditions, ensuring that products are not made with forced labour or in environmentally damaging ways.⁴⁶ AI can further enhance these efforts by analysing satellite imagery, social media, and other data sources to monitor compliance with ethical standards.⁴⁷

However, the implementation of these technologies is not without challenges. Small producers in developing countries may lack the resources or technical capacity to participate in blockchain-based systems.⁴⁸ Moreover, the data collected by these systems can be used for surveillance, raising concerns about privacy and power imbalances.⁴⁹ Any implementation must therefore be sensitive to the social and economic contexts in which supply chains operate, ensuring that the benefits are shared equitably.⁵⁰

### 5.3.3. Automation and Efficiency

AI-driven automation has the potential to revolutionise supply chain management, reducing costs, improving speed, and enhancing accuracy.⁵¹ From autonomous vehicles and robotic warehouses to predictive maintenance and demand forecasting, the applications are vast.⁵² When integrated with blockchain, these systems can create a seamless, transparent, and secure flow of goods and information.⁵³

Yet automation also raises concerns about employment and social impact. The displacement of workers by machines is a growing concern, particularly in sectors like logistics and manufacturing.⁵⁴ Moreover, the concentration of power in the hands of a few large corporations that control these technologies could exacerbate inequalities.⁵⁵ Addressing these challenges requires proactive policies and investments in education and retraining, as well as a commitment to ensuring that the benefits of automation are broadly shared.⁵⁶

---

## 5.4. Governance and Public Services

The application of blockchain and AI to governance and public services holds the promise of more efficient, transparent, and inclusive systems. From voting and identity management to social welfare and urban planning, these technologies have the potential to transform how societies are organised and how citizens interact with the state.⁵⁷

### 5.4.1. Digital Identity and Voting

Blockchain-based digital identity systems can provide individuals with secure, portable, and self-sovereign control over their personal information.⁵⁸ This can be particularly valuable for refugees, stateless persons, and others who lack official documentation, enabling access to services and participation in civic life.⁵⁹

Similarly, blockchain can enhance the integrity of voting systems by providing a transparent and tamper-proof record of votes.⁶⁰ When combined with AI for voter authentication and fraud detection, these systems can increase participation and confidence in democratic processes.⁶¹ However, the implementation of such systems must be careful to address concerns about privacy, accessibility, and the potential for coercion or manipulation.⁶²

### 5.4.2. Public Administration and Service Delivery

AI can improve the efficiency and responsiveness of public administration by automating routine tasks, analysing large datasets to inform policy decisions, and providing personalised services to citizens.⁶³ Blockchain can enhance transparency and accountability by creating immutable records of government transactions and decisions.⁶⁴

For example, blockchain-based systems can streamline the distribution of social welfare benefits, reducing fraud and ensuring that resources reach those who need them most.⁶⁵ AI can further enhance these efforts by identifying patterns of need and targeting interventions accordingly.⁶⁶ However, the use of AI in public services also raises concerns about bias, surveillance, and the erosion of human judgement.⁶⁷ It is essential to ensure that these technologies are deployed in ways that respect human rights and dignity.⁶⁸

### 5.4.3. Smart Cities and Urban Planning

The concept of the "smart city" relies heavily on the integration of AI and blockchain to manage urban infrastructure, from traffic and energy to waste and public safety.⁶⁹ AI can optimise the use of resources, predict maintenance needs, and improve the quality of life for residents.⁷⁰ Blockchain can provide a secure and transparent platform for managing the vast amounts of data generated by urban systems.⁷¹

However, the smart city vision also raises concerns about privacy, autonomy, and the concentration of power. The pervasive monitoring of urban life can create a climate of surveillance, while the algorithmic management of public services can reduce citizens to data points.⁷² Moreover, the benefits of smart city technologies may not be equally distributed, potentially exacerbating existing inequalities.⁷³ A human-centred approach to urban planning, which prioritises the needs and values of residents, is essential to ensure that these technologies serve the common good.⁷⁴
